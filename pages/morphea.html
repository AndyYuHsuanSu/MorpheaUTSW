Morphea

A localized form of sclerosing skin disease affecting the skin and tissue beneath — but not the internal organs. Here is what our specialist wants you to know.

Educational use only — not medical advice. Always consult your clinician for personal medical decisions.

What is Morphea?

Morphea is a condition in which the skin becomes hardened and thickened in localized patches. Unlike systemic scleroderma, morphea stays in the skin — it does not typically affect the heart, lungs, or other internal organs.

Subtypes

There are several subtypes of morphea — plaque, linear, generalized, and others. Knowing your specific subtype helps your care team plan monitoring and treatment. Ask your clinician: "What subtype of morphea do I have?"

What to Watch For

Changes in the size, color, or texture of existing patches, or new patches appearing, are worth reporting to your clinician. Some subtypes — especially linear morphea near a joint — may benefit from earlier intervention.
